Some categories are unusual and need specialized handling. Namely:

  • Mathematical categories (or NumericalElements), or categories in which items have a mathematical relationship between one another, which is used in the puzzle clues (items can be unevenly spaced as in the examples given):
    • numerical:
      • age
      • quantities (2,3,5,6,7)
      • years (1990, 1992, 1993, 1995, 1996)
      • office/house number
      • prices (various currencies)
      • weight
      • score/marks
      • percentage
    • times: e.g. 8:15, 8:45, 9:45, 10:15, 10:45
      • subject to next day calculation in some problems (00:30 is an hour after 23:30)
    • ordinals:textual items for which there is a mathematical relationship between the numerical representation of the items
      • days: e.g. Saturday, Sunday, Tuesday, Thursday, Friday (needs to be interpreted as 1-7 correctly)
        • subject to optional wrap-around in some problems; if the days are not fixed in time, arguably, Sunday can be the day after Saturday.
      • months: e.g. January, March, June, July, August (needs to be converted to 1-12 correctly)
        • subject to optional wrap-around in some problems; if the months are not fixed in time, arguably, January can be the month after December.
      • ranks: e.g. lieutenant, captain, lieutenant-colonel, general
      • ranking: first, third, fourth, fifth, sixth
    • dates: 14 July, 17 August, 18 November, 1 December, ...
  • text properties of the item used in clues
    • word length (e.g. name with 7 letters)
    • word spelling
  • repeating items:
    • within a category (e.g. two students prefer blue, two prefer red, and two prefer orange)
    • across categories (e.g. pet's names & owner's names have the same items)
  • relationship across categories (e.g. both animals and parcels have weights and a clue reads like Lucie's dog weights twice as much as the parcel given to Paul)
  • a combination of types
    • e.g. (mathematical & repeating items) two of the cost items are equal at $80, two other equal at $90, two other equal at $100.
    • e.g. (relationship across categories & text properties) dog's name's first letter is not the same as the owner's name's first letter

For many of these special categories, it seems the best approach is to maintain a "shadow" value property (in addition to the text property), which is used for computations. A translator will convert the text of the item into a mathematical entity. The shadow value could be generated once and stored, or computed on the fly each time it is needed.
